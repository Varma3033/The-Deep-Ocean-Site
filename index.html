The Sunlight Zone

(AKA the Epipelagic zone)

Depth: Surface to 200m

In this zone, the sun still shines through, which is why most of the marine life we know exists here.

What you might find here (click for a fun fact):

Phytoplankton produce around 50% of Earth's Oxygen and remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

Phytoplankton

Dolphins are highly intelligent and can communicate through echo-location and body language.

Dolphin

Not much is known about the lives of young sea turtles, due to tracking difficulty and they can take up to 20 years to resurface.

Sea Turtle

You would also find Zooplankton, which feed on phytoplankton and are in turn fed on by fish and sharks and even whales, which is important in the marine food chain.

The surface temperature in this layer can range from 36°C in the persian gulf, to -2°C near the north pole.

Dive Deeper

The Twilight Zone

(AKA the Mesopelagic zone)

Depth: 200m to 1000m

In this zone, light becomes dimmer and the lack of solar energy means that animals must find other methods to survive and adapt.

What you might find here:

Whales have their own 'dialect', using specific sounds and songs to help differentiate who is and isn't family.

Whales

Octopuses have 3 hearts, 2 for blood circulation to its gills and 1 for circulation to its organs and muscles.

Octopus

Jellyfish dont have brains, hearts or lungs and are made of just 3 layers which help easily digest and absorb nutrients.

Jellyfish

About 90% of the worlds fish by weight also live here - 10 billion tons.

The deepest freedive recorded by a human in history was at 250m, which is crazy considering the pressure in this layer is 26 times greater than at the surface, which would crush most human lungs.

Even Deeper

The Midnight Zone

(AKA the Mesopelagic zone)

Depth: 1000m to 4000m

In this zone, it is completely dark and food is scarce, the majority of animals have adapted with bioluminescence in order to hunt and see.

What you might find here:

Anglerfish have a rod on their heads that contains bioluminescent bacteria which attracts prey.

Anglerfish

Viperfish have bioluminescent/silvery organs and giant, difficult to see teeth, used to lure prey and easily trap them.

Viperfish

These "eels" have a pelican-like mouth trapping prey and emit a red glow which is strange as deep see creatures supposedly cannot see red light.

Pelican Eel

The pressure here is over 110 times that at sea level.

Many species have slow metabolisms, relying on leftovers from above.

Average Depth of the Ocean:

~3682 meters

Further

The Abyss

(AKA the Abyssopelagic zone)

Depth: 4000m to 6000m

In this zone, the pressure is 600 times more than at sea level and hence several unique and highly specialised life forms can be found.

What you might find here:

Vampire squids dont have ink, instead they release sticky, bioluminescent mucus to scare off predators.

Vampire Squid

Sea pigs have pinky/near transparent bodies with no eyes, and are hard to obtain as when removed from their habitat, they disintegrate.

Sea Pigs

These sea spiders have long stilt like legs which they use to breathe, carry eggs and contains their digestive system.

Giant Sea Spider

Like the midnight zone, creatures living in the abyss rely on food from above and only about 5% of surface food will reach.

Whales falls, when whales pass away, are a massive source of food at this depth, with even their bones being eaten.

Almost There

The Trenches

(AKA the Hadalpelagic zone)

Depth: 6000m to 11000m

In this zone, the worlds deepest trenches, like the mariana trench, are found, with pressure ranging up to 1100 times that of surface level.

What you might find here:

Snailfish are the deepest ever recorded sea creatures, with tadpole shaped, slimy bodies that are suprisingly not bioluminescent.

Snailfish

Cusk Eels have sensory pores, translucent skin and a skeleton made with extra bone material, made to withstand immense pressure.

Cusk Eels

Amphipods are prominent in the trenches and are small, soft shelled crustaceans, known for being scavengers and eating each other.

Amphipods

Only very little is actualy known about this zone.

Scientist believe their are thousands of undiscovered species here.

Deepest discovered point of the Ocean:

Challenger Deep ~10,935 meters
